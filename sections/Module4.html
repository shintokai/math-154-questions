4.1 Core Concepts

Definition — Derivative

The derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\), denoted \(f'(a)\), is defined by

\[ f'(a) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} = \lim_{x\to a} \frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}, \] if the limit exists.

When the above limit exists, the function \(f(x)\) is said to be differentiable at \(x=a\). When the limit does not exist, we say \(f(x)\) is not differentiable at \(x=a\).

Theorem — Differentiation Rules

Let \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) be differentiable functions and let \(c\in\mathbb{R}\). Then:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[c f(x)] = c f'(x), \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[f(x)\pm g(x)] = f'(x)\pm g'(x), \]

Product Rule: \[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(x)g(x)] = f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x), \]

Quotient Rule: \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{f'(x)g(x)-f(x)g'(x)}{(g(x))^2}. \]

Note: the derivative of the quotient exists only at points where \(g(x)\neq 0\).

Theorem — Chain Rule

Let \(f\) and \(g\) be differentiable functions. Then the composition \(y=f(g(x))\) is differentiable, and

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x). \]

Proposition — Derivative Rules

The following are standard derivative formulas:

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0,\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[mx+b] = m,\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1},\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x,\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x,\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x},\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x,\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[b^x] = b^x \ln b,\\ &\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}. \end{aligned} \]

Theorem — Higher-order Derivatives

The second derivative of \(f(x)\) is \[ f''(x) = \frac{d^2}{dx^2}f(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f'(x+h)-f'(x)}{h}. \]

The n-th derivative of \(f(x)\) is \[ f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{d^n}{dx^n}f(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f^{(n-1)}(x+h)-f^{(n-1)}(x)}{h}. \]

Method — Implicit Differentiation

When \(y\) is given implicitly as a function of \(x\), differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\) and then solve the resulting equation for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).

Method — Logarithmic Differentiation
  1. Take the natural logarithm on both sides of the equation, and use log properties to rewrite any “complicated expression” as a sum of simpler terms.
  2. Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\).
  3. Solve the resulting equation for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).
Method — Steps to Find a Tangent Line

To find the tangent line at the point \((a,b)\):

  1. Find the first derivative \(f'(x)\).
  2. Compute \(f'(a)\); this is the slope of the tangent line at \(x=a\).
  3. Plug \(a\), \(b\), and \(f'(a)\) into the point-slope form: \[ y-b = f'(a)(x-a). \]
  4. Rearrange to slope–intercept form \(y=mx+b\) if desired.

Easy ★

  1. Module 4 — Easy — 1
    Find the derivatives of the following functions.
    1. \(f(x)=(x^3-1)(x+1)\).
      Answer

      \(f'(x)=4x^3+3x^2-1.\)

      Full Solution

      Use the product rule on \(f(x)=(x^3-1)(x+1)\).

      \[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= (x^3-1)\,\frac{d}{dx}(x+1) + (x+1)\,\frac{d}{dx}(x^3-1)\\[4pt] &= (x^3-1)\cdot 1 + (x+1)\cdot 3x^2\\[4pt] &= (x^3-1) + 3x^2(x+1)\\[4pt] &= (x^3-1) + 3x^2x + 3x^2\\[4pt] &= x^3-1 + 3x^3+3x^2\\[4pt] &= 4x^3+3x^2-1. \end{aligned} \]

    2. \(\displaystyle f(x)=\frac{\sqrt{x}+1}{x^2+1}\).
      Answer

      \[ f'(x) =\frac{\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}(x^2+1)-(\sqrt{x}+1)\cdot 2x}{(x^2+1)^2}. \]

      Full Solution

      Write the function as a quotient and apply the quotient rule.

      \[ f(x)=\frac{u(x)}{v(x)},\quad u(x)=\sqrt{x}+1,\quad v(x)=x^2+1. \]

      Then \[ u'(x)=\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}},\qquad v'(x)=2x. \]

      By the quotient rule, \[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &=\frac{u'(x)v(x)-u(x)v'(x)}{[v(x)]^2}\\[4pt] &=\frac{\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}(x^2+1)-(\sqrt{x}+1)\cdot 2x}{(x^2+1)^2}. \end{aligned} \]

    3. \(\displaystyle f(x)=(3x^2-1)\left(x^2-\frac{1}{x}\right).\)
      Answer

      \[ f'(x) =6x\left(x^2-\frac{1}{x}\right) +(3x^2-1)\left(2x+\frac{1}{x^2}\right). \]

      Full Solution

      Again use the product rule. Let \[ u(x)=3x^2-1,\qquad v(x)=x^2-\frac{1}{x}. \]

      Then \[ u'(x)=6x,\qquad v'(x)=2x+\frac{1}{x^2}. \]

      By the product rule, \[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &=u'(x)v(x)+u(x)v'(x)\\[4pt] &=6x\left(x^2-\frac{1}{x}\right) +(3x^2-1)\left(2x+\frac{1}{x^2}\right). \end{aligned} \]

    4. \(f(x)=\ln(x^{154})\).
      Answer

      \[ f'(x)=\frac{154}{x}. \]

      Full Solution

      Method 1 (chain rule).

      \[ \begin{aligned} f(x) &=\ln(x^{154}),\\[4pt] f'(x) &=\frac{1}{x^{154}}\cdot\frac{d}{dx}(x^{154}) =\frac{1}{x^{154}}\cdot 154x^{153}\\[4pt] &=154\frac{x^{153}}{x^{154}} =\frac{154}{x}. \end{aligned} \]

      Method 2 (logarithm law).

      Using \(\ln(x^{154})=154\ln x\), \[ \begin{aligned} f(x)&=154\ln x,\\[4pt] f'(x)&=154\cdot\frac{1}{x} =\frac{154}{x}. \end{aligned} \]

    5. \(f(t)=\ln(3t+1)\).
      Answer

      \[ f'(t)=\frac{3}{3t+1}. \]

      Full Solution

      Use the chain rule with \(\ln(\,\cdot\,)\).

      Let \(u(t)=3t+1\). Then \(f(t)=\ln u(t)\) and \[ f'(t)=\frac{1}{u(t)}\cdot u'(t) =\frac{1}{3t+1}\cdot 3 =\frac{3}{3t+1}. \]

    6. \(\displaystyle f(t)=\ln\bigl(\sqrt{t^2-1}\bigr).\)
      Answer

      \[ f'(t)=\frac{t}{t^2-1}. \]

      Full Solution

      We differentiate using the chain rule.

      Write \[ f(t)=\ln\bigl(\sqrt{t^2-1}\bigr). \] Let \(u(t)=\sqrt{t^2-1}\). Then \(f(t)=\ln (u(t))\), so \[ f'(t)=\frac{1}{u(t)}\cdot u'(t) =\frac{u'(t)}{\sqrt{t^2-1}}. \]

      Next compute \(u'(t)\). Since \(u(t)=(t^2-1)^{1/2}\), \[ \begin{aligned} u'(t) &=\frac{1}{2}(t^2-1)^{-1/2}\cdot\frac{d}{dt}(t^2-1)\\[4pt] &=\frac{1}{2}(t^2-1)^{-1/2}\cdot 2t\\[4pt] &=\frac{t}{\sqrt{t^2-1}}. \end{aligned} \]

      Substitute into \(f'(t)=\dfrac{u'(t)}{\sqrt{t^2-1}}\): \[ \begin{aligned} f'(t) &=\frac{\dfrac{t}{\sqrt{t^2-1}}}{\sqrt{t^2-1}} =\frac{t}{(\sqrt{t^2-1})^2} =\frac{t}{t^2-1}. \end{aligned} \]

  2. Module 4 — Easy — 2
    Find an equation of the tangent line to the given curve at the given point.
    1. \(f(x)=\sqrt{2x-1}\) at \((5,3)\).
      Answer

      Tangent line: \(y=\dfrac{1}{3}x+\dfrac{4}{3}.\)

      Full Solution

      First compute the derivative of \(f(x)=\sqrt{2x-1}\).

      Rewrite \(f(x)=(2x-1)^{1/2}\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &=\frac{1}{2}(2x-1)^{-1/2}\cdot\frac{d}{dx}(2x-1)\\[4pt] &=\frac{1}{2}(2x-1)^{-1/2}\cdot 2\\[4pt] &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2x-1}}. \end{aligned} \]

      At \(x=5\), \[ f'(5)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\cdot 5-1}} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{9}} =\frac{1}{3}. \]

      The slope of the tangent line at \((5,3)\) is therefore \(m=f'(5)=\dfrac{1}{3}\). Using the point–slope form, \[ \begin{aligned} y-3 &= \frac{1}{3}(x-5),\\[4pt] y &= 3+\frac{1}{3}(x-5)\\[4pt] &=\frac{1}{3}x+\frac{4}{3}. \end{aligned} \]

    2. \(f(x)=e^x\) at \((0,1)\).
      Answer

      Tangent line: \(y=x+1.\)

      Full Solution

      The derivative of \(f(x)=e^x\) is \[ f'(x)=e^x. \]

      At \(x=0\), \[ f'(0)=e^0=1. \] Hence the tangent line at \((0,1)\) has slope \(m=1\).

      Use the point–slope form at \((0,1)\): \[ \begin{aligned} y-1 &= 1\cdot(x-0),\\[4pt] y &= x+1. \end{aligned} \]

  3. Module 4 — Easy — 3
    Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for each of the following equations.
    1. \(x^2y^3-xy=10.\)
      Answer

      \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{-2xy^3+y}{3x^2y^2-x}. \]

      Full Solution

      Differentiate implicitly with respect to \(x\).

      Start from \[ x^2y^3-xy=10. \] Differentiate term by term: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx}(x^2y^3)-\frac{d}{dx}(xy) &=\frac{d}{dx}(10). \end{aligned} \]

      For the first term, use the product rule: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx}(x^2y^3) &=2x\cdot y^3 + x^2\cdot 3y^2\,\frac{dy}{dx}. \end{aligned} \] For the second term, \[ \frac{d}{dx}(xy)=y+x\frac{dy}{dx}. \] The right-hand side derivative is \(0\).

      Putting these together, \[ \begin{aligned} 2xy^3+3x^2y^2\frac{dy}{dx} -\bigl(y+x\frac{dy}{dx}\bigr) &=0. \end{aligned} \]

      Group the terms involving \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\): \[ \begin{aligned} 3x^2y^2\frac{dy}{dx}-x\frac{dy}{dx} &= -2xy^3 + y,\\[4pt] \left(3x^2y^2-x\right)\frac{dy}{dx} &= -2xy^3 + y. \end{aligned} \]

      Finally, \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{-2xy^3+y}{3x^2y^2-x}. \]

    2. \(x^3y^5+3x=8y^3+1.\)
      Answer

      \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{3x^2y^5+3}{24y^2-5x^3y^4}. \]

      Full Solution

      Differentiate implicitly with respect to \(x\).

      \[ x^3y^5+3x=8y^3+1. \]

      Differentiate each term: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx}(x^3y^5)+\frac{d}{dx}(3x) &=\frac{d}{dx}(8y^3)+\frac{d}{dx}(1). \end{aligned} \]

      For \(x^3y^5\), use the product rule: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx}(x^3y^5) &=3x^2y^5 + x^3\cdot 5y^4\frac{dy}{dx}. \end{aligned} \] Also, \[ \frac{d}{dx}(3x)=3,\qquad \frac{d}{dx}(8y^3)=8\cdot 3y^2\frac{dy}{dx}=24y^2\frac{dy}{dx}. \]

      Thus \[ \begin{aligned} 3x^2y^5 + x^3\cdot 5y^4\frac{dy}{dx} + 3 &= 24y^2\frac{dy}{dx}. \end{aligned} \]

      Collect the \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms on one side: \[ \begin{aligned} x^3\cdot 5y^4\frac{dy}{dx} - 24y^2\frac{dy}{dx} &= -3x^2y^5 - 3,\\[4pt] \left(5x^3y^4 - 24y^2\right)\frac{dy}{dx} &= -3x^2y^5 -3. \end{aligned} \]

      Multiply numerator and denominator by \(-1\) for a cleaner form: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{3x^2y^5+3}{24y^2-5x^3y^4}. \end{aligned} \]

    3. \(y=\sin x+\cos y.\)
      Answer

      \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{\cos x}{1+\sin y}. \]

      Full Solution

      Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\).

      \[ y=\sin x+\cos y. \]

      Taking derivatives, \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dx}(y) &=\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x)+\frac{d}{dx}(\cos y). \end{aligned} \]

      \[ \frac{d}{dx}(y)=\frac{dy}{dx},\qquad \frac{d}{dx}(\sin x)=\cos x. \] For \(\cos y\), use the chain rule: \[ \frac{d}{dx}(\cos y)=-\sin y\cdot \frac{dy}{dx}. \]

      Thus, \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx} &=\cos x -\sin y\,\frac{dy}{dx}. \end{aligned} \]

      Collect \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms: \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx}+\sin y\,\frac{dy}{dx} &=\cos x,\\[4pt] (1+\sin y)\frac{dy}{dx} &=\cos x. \end{aligned} \]

      Therefore, \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{\cos x}{1+\sin y}. \]

  4. Module 4 — Easy — 4
    Consider the function \(f(x)=3x^2+4x+30\). Compute the derivative of \(f(x)\) when \(x=1\) by first finding \(f'(x)\) using the differentiation rules and then evaluating it at \(x=1\).
    Answer

    \(f'(1)=10.\)

    Full Solution

    Differentiate the polynomial term by term.

    \[ \begin{aligned} f(x)&=3x^2+4x+30,\\[4pt] f'(x)&=6x+4+0=6x+4. \end{aligned} \]

    Now evaluate at \(x=1\): \[ f'(1)=6\cdot 1+4=6+4=10. \]

  1. Module 4 — Intermediate — 1
    Consider the function \(f(x)=3x^2+4x+30\). Compute the derivative of \(f(x)\) using the (limit) definition of the derivative and evaluate it at \(x=1\). Hint: It may be easier to use \[ f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}. \]
    Answer

    \(f'(x)=6x+4\), so \(f'(1)=10\).

    Full Solution

    By the definition of the derivative given in the hint,

    \[ f'(a)=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}. \]

    Here \(f(x)=3x^2+4x+30\), so \(f(a)=3a^2+4a+30\). Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} f'(a) &=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{3x^2+4x+30-(3a^2+4a+30)}{x-a}\\[4pt] &=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{3x^2-3a^2+4x-4a}{x-a}\\[4pt] &=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{3(x^2-a^2)+4(x-a)}{x-a}\\[4pt] &=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{3(x-a)(x+a)+4(x-a)}{x-a}\\[4pt] &=\lim_{x\to a}\bigl(3(x+a)+4\bigr)\\[4pt] &=3(a+a)+4\\[2pt] &=6a+4. \end{aligned} \]

    Therefore, as a function of \(x\), \(f'(x)=6x+4\). At \(x=1\), \[ f'(1)=6(1)+4=10. \]

    Note: This agrees with the answer obtained in Easy (\(\star\)), Question 4.

  2. Module 4 — Intermediate — 2
    Find the derivatives of the following functions:
    1. \(f(x)=\sin(x^5+7x+3)\)
      Answer

      \(f'(x)=\cos(x^5+7x+3)\,(5x^4+7)\).

      Full Solution

      Let \(u(x)=x^5+7x+3\). Then \(f(x)=\sin(u)\). By the chain rule,

      \[ f'(x)=\cos(u)\,u'(x). \]

      Since \(u'(x)=5x^4+7\), we obtain \[ f'(x)=\cos(x^5+7x+3)\,(5x^4+7). \]

    2. \(f(x)=\ln(x-9)\cos(e^x)\)
      Answer

      \(f'(x)=\displaystyle \frac{1}{x-9}\cos(e^x)-\ln(x-9)\,\sin(e^x)\,e^x.\)

      Full Solution

      The function is a product of \(\ln(x-9)\) and \(\cos(e^x)\). Use the product rule:

      \[ f'(x)=\bigl(\ln(x-9)\bigr)' \cos(e^x) +\ln(x-9)\,\bigl(\cos(e^x)\bigr)'. \]

      Compute each derivative: \[ \bigl(\ln(x-9)\bigr)'=\frac{1}{x-9},\qquad \bigl(\cos(e^x)\bigr)'=-\sin(e^x)\,e^x \] (chain rule on \(\cos(e^x)\)).

      Substituting, \[ f'(x)=\frac{1}{x-9}\cos(e^x)-\ln(x-9)\,\sin(e^x)\,e^x. \]

    3. \(f(x)=\ln\left(\dfrac{\cos(3x+1)}{x^3-2}\right)\)
      Answer

      \[ f'(x)= -3\tan(3x+1)-\frac{3x^2}{x^3-2}. \]

      Full Solution

      First simplify using logarithm laws:

      \[ f(x)=\ln(\cos(3x+1))-\ln(x^3-2). \]

      Differentiate term by term. For the first term, \[ \frac{d}{dx}\ln(\cos(3x+1)) =\frac{1}{\cos(3x+1)}\cdot\frac{d}{dx}\cos(3x+1) =\frac{-\sin(3x+1)\cdot 3}{\cos(3x+1)} =-3\tan(3x+1). \]

      For the second term, \[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(-\ln(x^3-2)\bigr) =-\frac{1}{x^3-2}\cdot 3x^2 =-\frac{3x^2}{x^3-2}. \]

      Therefore \[ f'(x)=-3\tan(3x+1)-\frac{3x^2}{x^3-2}. \]

  3. Module 4 — Intermediate — 3
    Find an equation of the tangent line to the given curve at the given point.
    1. \(f(x)=x\cos x\) at \((0,0)\)
      Answer

      \(y=x\).

      Full Solution

      We first compute \(f'(x)\) using the product rule:

      \[ f'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}(x)\cos x+x\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) =1\cdot\cos x+x(-\sin x) =\cos x-x\sin x. \]

      At \(x=0\), \[ f'(0)=\cos 0-0\cdot\sin 0=1. \] Thus the slope of the tangent line at \((0,0)\) is \(1\).

      Using point–slope form, \[ y-0=1(x-0)\quad\Rightarrow\quad y=x. \]

    2. \(f(x)=\ln(3x^2-11)-5x\) at \((2,-10)\)
      Answer

      \(y=7x-24\).

      Full Solution

      First verify that the point lies on the curve:

      \[ f(2)=\ln(3\cdot 2^2-11)-5\cdot 2 =\ln(12-11)-10 =\ln 1-10 =0-10=-10. \] So \((2,-10)\) is indeed on the graph.

      Now differentiate \(f(x)\): \[ f'(x)=\frac{1}{3x^2-11}\cdot 6x-5=\frac{6x}{3x^2-11}-5. \]

      At \(x=2\), \[ f'(2)=\frac{6\cdot 2}{3\cdot 2^2-11}-5 =\frac{12}{12-11}-5 =12-5=7. \] Thus the slope of the tangent line at \((2,-10)\) is \(7\).

      Using point–slope form, \[ y+10=7(x-2). \] Rearranging, \[ y=7x-24. \]

  4. Module 4 — Intermediate — 4
    Find an equation of the tangent line to the graph of \(f(x)=\sqrt{x^2+3}\) at the point where \(x=1\).
    Answer

    \(y=\dfrac{x+3}{2}\).

    Full Solution

    First find the corresponding \(y\)-coordinate: \[ f(1)=\sqrt{1^2+3}=\sqrt{4}=2, \] so the point of tangency is \((1,2)\).

    Next find the derivative: \[ f(x)=(x^2+3)^{1/2},\quad f'(x)=\frac{1}{2}(x^2+3)^{-1/2}\cdot 2x =\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+3}}. \]

    At \(x=1\), \[ f'(1)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1^2+3}}=\frac{1}{2}. \]

    Using point–slope form at \((1,2)\), \[ y-2=\frac{1}{2}(x-1). \] Rearranging, \[ y=2+\frac{1}{2}(x-1) =2+\frac{x}{2}-\frac{1}{2} =\frac{x+3}{2}. \]

  5. Module 4 — Intermediate — 5
    Is \(f(x)=|x-10|\) differentiable at \(x=10\)? If so, find its derivative at \(x=10\) using the definition of the derivative. If not, show that it is not differentiable at \(x=10\).
    Answer

    \(f(x)=|x-10|\) is not differentiable at \(x=10\).

    Full Solution

    We examine the derivative at \(x=10\) using the limit definition: \[ f'(10)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(10+h)-f(10)}{h}. \]

    Since \(f(x)=|x-10|\), we have \(f(10)=|0|=0\) and \[ f(10+h)=|10+h-10|=|h|. \] Thus \[ \frac{f(10+h)-f(10)}{h}=\frac{|h|}{h}. \]

    Right-hand limit (\(h\to 0^+\)):

    For \(h>0\), \(|h|=h\), so \[ \lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{|h|}{h}=\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{h}{h}=1. \]

    Left-hand limit (\(h\to 0^-\)):

    For \(h<0\), \(|h|=-h\), so \[ \lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{|h|}{h} =\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{-h}{h} =-1. \]

    Since \[ \lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{|h|}{h}=1\neq -1=\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{|h|}{h}, \] the two one-sided limits are not equal. Therefore the limit \(\displaystyle \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(10+h)-f(10)}{h}\) does not exist, and \(f\) is not differentiable at \(x=10\).

  6. Module 4 — Intermediate — 6
    Find \(f^{(n)}(x)\) for the given \(f(x)\) and \(n\):
    1. \(f(x)=4x^2-2x+1\) for \(n=2\)
      Answer

      \(f''(x)=8.\)

      Full Solution

      First derivative: \[ f'(x)=\frac{d}{dx}(4x^2-2x+1)=8x-2. \]

      Second derivative: \[ f''(x)=\frac{d}{dx}(8x-2)=8. \]

    2. \(f(x)=x^4-2x^3+6x^2-3x+10\) for \(n=2\)
      Answer

      \(f''(x)=12x^2-12x+12.\)

      Full Solution

      Compute the first derivative term by term:

      \[ f'(x)=4x^3-6x^2+12x-3. \]

      Differentiate again: \[ f''(x)=\frac{d}{dx}(4x^3-6x^2+12x-3) =12x^2-12x+12. \]

    3. \(f(x)=\dfrac{x}{2x+1}\) for \(n=2\)
      Answer

      \(f''(x)=-\dfrac{4}{(2x+1)^3}.\)

      Full Solution

      Use the quotient rule. Let \(u=x\) and \(v=2x+1\). Then \[ u'=1,\qquad v'=2. \]

      The first derivative is \[ f'(x)=\frac{u'v-uv'}{v^2} =\frac{(1)(2x+1)-x\cdot 2}{(2x+1)^2} =\frac{2x+1-2x}{(2x+1)^2} =\frac{1}{(2x+1)^2}. \]

      Now differentiate \(f'(x)=(2x+1)^{-2}\): \[ f''(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\bigl((2x+1)^{-2}\bigr) =-2(2x+1)^{-3}\cdot 2 =-\frac{4}{(2x+1)^3}. \]

  7. Module 4 — Intermediate — 7
    Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for the following equations:
    1. \(e^{2x+3y}=x^2-\ln(xy^3)\)
      Answer

      \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{2x-\frac{1}{x}-2e^{2x+3y}}{3e^{2x+3y}+\frac{3}{y}}. \]

      Full Solution

      Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\).

      Left-hand side: \[ \frac{d}{dx}e^{2x+3y} =e^{2x+3y}\cdot(2+3y'). \]

      Right-hand side: \[ \frac{d}{dx}\bigl(x^2-\ln(xy^3)\bigr) =2x-\frac{d}{dx}\bigl(\ln(xy^3)\bigr). \]

      For \(\ln(xy^3)\), \[ \frac{d}{dx}(xy^3)=y^3+3xy^2y', \] so \[ \frac{d}{dx}\ln(xy^3) =\frac{y^3+3xy^2y'}{xy^3} =\frac{1}{x}+\frac{3y'}{y}. \]

      Hence the right-hand side derivative is \[ 2x-\left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{3y'}{y}\right). \]

      Set derivatives equal: \[ e^{2x+3y}(2+3y')=2x-\frac{1}{x}-\frac{3y'}{y}. \]

      Expand and collect terms in \(y'\): \[ 2e^{2x+3y}+3e^{2x+3y}y' =2x-\frac{1}{x}-\frac{3y'}{y}, \] \[ 3e^{2x+3y}y'+\frac{3y'}{y} =2x-\frac{1}{x}-2e^{2x+3y}. \]

      Factor \(y'\): \[ y'\left(3e^{2x+3y}+\frac{3}{y}\right) =2x-\frac{1}{x}-2e^{2x+3y}. \]

      Therefore \[ \frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{2x-\frac{1}{x}-2e^{2x+3y}}{3e^{2x+3y}+\frac{3}{y}}. \]

    2. \(x^2+y^2=25\) at \((3,-4)\)
      Answer

      \[ \frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{(3,-4)}=\frac{3}{4}. \]

      Full Solution

      Differentiate implicitly: \[ \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)+\frac{d}{dx}(y^2)=0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2x+2yy'=0. \]

      Solve for \(y'\): \[ 2yy'=-2x,\qquad y'=-\frac{x}{y}. \]

      At \((x,y)=(3,-4)\), \[ y'=-\frac{3}{-4}=\frac{3}{4}. \]

    3. \(x^2+3xy+y^2=-1\) at \((-1,1)\)
      Answer

      \[ \frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{(-1,1)}=1. \]

      Full Solution

      Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\):

      \[ \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)+\frac{d}{dx}(3xy)+\frac{d}{dx}(y^2)=0. \]

      This gives \[ 2x+3(xy'+y)+2yy'=0. \] Expanding, \[ 2x+3xy'+3y+2yy'=0. \]

      Group the \(y'\)-terms: \[ (3x+2y)y'=-2x-3y. \] Hence \[ y'=\frac{-2x-3y}{3x+2y}. \]

      At \((x,y)=(-1,1)\), \[ y'=\frac{-2(-1)-3\cdot 1}{3(-1)+2\cdot 1} =\frac{2-3}{-3+2} =\frac{-1}{-1} =1. \]

    Use logarithmic differentiation to find the derivative of \[ y = x^{x+2}. \]

    \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}=\left(\ln x+\frac{x+2}{x}\right)x^{x+2}.\)

    Consider a manufacturing company whose average cost function (in dollars) for producing \(q\) units is \[ \bar C(q) = q^2 - 8q + 40. \] Use this information to determine the marginal cost function.
    Answer

    Marginal cost: \(C'(q)=3q^2-16q+40.\)

    Full Solution

  10. Module 4 — Intermediate — 10
    A small manufacturing company produces a widget. The cost (in dollars) to produce \(q\) units is \[ C(q)=0.02q^3 - 0.8q^2 + 15q + 500. \]
    1. Find the marginal cost function.
    2. Estimate the cost of producing the \(101\)-st widget using the marginal cost function.
    3. Estimate the cost of producing the \(101\)-st widget using a linear approximation.
    Answer

    (a) \(C'(q)=0.06q^2-1.6q+15.\)

    (b) Approx. cost of the 101st widget \(\approx C'(100)=\$455.\)

    (c) Using linear approximation, the estimated cost of the 101st widget is again \(\$455.\)

    Full Solution

    (a) The marginal cost is the derivative of the cost function.

    \[ C'(q)=\frac{d}{dq}\bigl(0.02q^3-0.8q^2+15q+500\bigr) =0.06q^2-1.6q+15. \]

    (b) The marginal cost at \(q=100\) approximates the cost of producing the 101st widget:

    \[ \begin{aligned} C'(100) &=0.06(100)^2-1.6(100)+15\\ &=0.06\cdot 10000-160+15\\ &=600-160+15\\ &=455. \end{aligned} \]

    So the 101st widget costs approximately \(\$455\).

    (c) Linear approximation.

    A linear approximation of \(C(q)\) at \(q=100\) is \[ L(q)=C(100)+C'(100)(q-100). \]

    First compute \(C(100)\): \[ \begin{aligned} C(100) &=0.02(100)^3-0.8(100)^2+15(100)+500\\ &=0.02\cdot 1\,000\,000-0.8\cdot 10\,000+1500+500\\ &=20\,000-8\,000+1\,500+500\\ &=14\,000. \end{aligned} \]

    We already found \(C'(100)=455\), so \[ L(q)=14\,000+455(q-100). \]

    To estimate the total cost of producing 101 widgets, set \(q=101\): \[ C(101)\approx L(101) =14\,000+455(101-100) =14\,000+455 =14\,455. \]

    The approximate cost of the 101st widget is then \[ C(101)-C(100)\approx 14\,455-14\,000=455, \] in agreement with the marginal cost estimate.

  11. Module 4 — Intermediate — 11
    A tailor is currently producing 80 suits per month and sells them for \$100 per suit. The monthly demand curve is given by \(q = 100 - 2\sqrt{p}\). Find the current price elasticity of demand \(E_D\), and decide whether the price should be raised or lowered to increase revenue.
    Answer

    \(\displaystyle E_D=\frac{1}{8}\) (inelastic). The tailor should increase the price to increase revenue.

    Full Solution

    The demand curve is \(q(p)=100-2\sqrt{p}\), where \(q\) is quantity and \(p\) is price.

    First find the current point: he produces 80 suits and charges \$100 per suit. Check consistency with the demand curve: \[ q(100)=100-2\sqrt{100}=100-2\cdot 10=80, \] so the point \((p,q)=(100,80)\) lies on the demand curve.

    Differentiate \(q(p)\) with respect to \(p\): \[ q(p)=100-2p^{1/2},\quad q'(p)=-2\cdot \frac{1}{2}p^{-1/2}=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{p}}. \]

    In many calculus texts, the price elasticity of demand is defined as \[ E_D(p)=-\frac{p}{q(p)}\,q'(p). \] Substituting \(q'(p)=-1/\sqrt{p}\), \[ E_D(p) =-\frac{p}{q(p)}\left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{p}}\right) =\frac{p}{q(p)}\cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{p}} =\frac{\sqrt{p}}{q(p)}. \]

    At \(p=100\), we have \(\sqrt{p}=10\) and \(q(100)=80\). Thus \[ E_D(100)=\frac{10}{80}=\frac{1}{8}. \]

    Since \(|E_D|=\frac{1}{8}<1\), demand is inelastic at the current price. For inelastic demand, raising the price increases total revenue (because the percentage drop in quantity demanded is smaller than the percentage increase in price). Therefore, the tailor should increase the price to increase revenue.

  1. Module 4 — Challenging — 1
    Find \(f^{(n)}(x)\) for the given \(f(x)\) and \(n\):
    1. \(f(x)=\ln x\) for \(n=5\)
      Answer

      \[ f^{(5)}(x)=\frac{24}{x^5}. \]

      Full Solution

      For \(f(x)=\ln x\), the derivatives follow a pattern:

      \[ \begin{aligned} f'(x)&=\frac{1}{x},\\ f''(x)&=-\frac{1}{x^2},\\ f^{(3)}(x)&=\frac{2!}{x^3},\\ f^{(4)}(x)&=-\frac{3!}{x^4},\\ f^{(5)}(x)&=\frac{4!}{x^5}. \end{aligned} \]

      More generally, \[ f^{(n)}(x)=(-1)^{n-1}\frac{(n-1)!}{x^n}. \] For \(n=5\), \[ f^{(5)}(x)=(-1)^4\frac{4!}{x^5}=\frac{24}{x^5}. \]

      Note. The symbol \(m!\) is called factorial. For a positive integer \(m\), \[ m! = 1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdots m, \] and by convention \(0! = 1\). For example, \(4! = 1\cdot 2\cdot 3\cdot 4 = 24\).

    2. \(f(x)=\sin(x+10)\) for \(n=20\)
      Answer

      \[ f^{(20)}(x)=\sin(x+10). \]

      Full Solution

      The derivatives of \(\sin(x+10)\) repeat with period \(4\):

      \[ \begin{aligned} f(x) &= \sin(x+10),\\ f'(x) &= \cos(x+10),\\ f''(x) &= -\sin(x+10),\\ f^{(3)}(x) &= -\cos(x+10),\\ f^{(4)}(x) &= \sin(x+10), \end{aligned} \]

      and then the cycle repeats every 4 derivatives.

      Since \(20\div 4=5\) with remainder \(0\), the 20th derivative is the same as the 4th derivative: \[ f^{(20)}(x)=f^{(4)}(x)=\sin(x+10). \]

    3. \(f(x)=e^{10x}\) for \(n=101\)
      Answer

      \[ f^{(101)}(x)=10^{101}e^{10x}. \]

      Full Solution

      Let \(f(x)=e^{10x}\). Using the chain rule,

      \[ \begin{aligned} f'(x)&=10e^{10x},\\ f''(x)&=10^2e^{10x},\\ f^{(3)}(x)&=10^3e^{10x}. \end{aligned} \]

      Each differentiation introduces another factor of \(10\). By the pattern, the \(n\)-th derivative is \[ f^{(n)}(x)=10^n e^{10x}. \]

      For \(n=101\), \[ f^{(101)}(x)=10^{101}e^{10x}. \]

    Very challenging! Find the equation of the tangent line to \[ y=\frac{5}{x} \] which passes through the point \((5,0)\). (Hint: the point \((5,0)\) is not on the curve \(y=\dfrac{5}{x}\).)
